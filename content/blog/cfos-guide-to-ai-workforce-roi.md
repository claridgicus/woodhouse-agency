Most CFOs approach AI agents the same way they approach software procurement: cost line, vendor risk, integration overhead. That framing misses the point.

An AI agent isn't a tool your team uses. It's a member of your team that scales without additional cost, never leaves for a competitor, and compounds in value as it learns your workflows.

Here's how to think about it properly.

## The headcount math your board already understands

A fully loaded senior knowledge worker — analyst, paralegal, finance associate — costs £80k–£120k per year when you include salary, National Insurance, benefits, office space, management overhead, and recruitment.

That employee works roughly 1,800 productive hours per year. Of those, our benchmarks across 40+ enterprise deployments suggest **55–70% are spent on tasks that are structurally automatable** with today's agent technology: research, drafting, data synthesis, classification, report generation, and routine decision-making against known rules.

The non-automatable remainder — stakeholder relationships, judgment calls under genuine uncertainty, creative strategy — is where human value concentrates.

So the ROI question is straightforward: what is the cost of automating the 60% versus the cost of hiring humans to do it?

## The agent cost structure

A well-deployed agent layer operating at enterprise scale costs:

- **Implementation**: £50k–£200k depending on complexity (one-time)
- **Ongoing**: £8k–£20k/month for monitoring, optimisation, and model costs

For a team of 10 analysts at fully loaded £95k each, that's £950k/year in workforce cost. If agents handle 65% of their workload autonomously, the value created is **£617k/year** — against an ongoing cost one-fifth of that.

Payback on the implementation cost typically lands between **3 and 7 months**.

## The compounding dynamic CFOs undervalue

Software depreciates. Agents appreciate.

With each month of production data, an agent layer improves its coverage rate, reduces its error rate, and handles a broader surface area of work. At month 1, you might see 50% autonomous coverage. By month 12, the same pipeline routinely exceeds 80%.

Human hires don't work this way. A new analyst takes 3–6 months to reach full productivity, and then plateaus.

Model the 3-year NPV of an agent deployment versus the 3-year cost of equivalent headcount, and the gap becomes dramatic.

## When the economics don't work

This isn't universal. Agents deliver poor ROI when:

- **Workflows are highly variable and poorly documented** — agents need defined processes to follow
- **Stakes are high and error tolerance is low** — surgical precision, legal liability, regulatory sign-off
- **Volume is insufficient** — the fixed implementation cost doesn't amortise across enough task volume
- **Human relationships are the product** — client-facing roles where trust is the asset

For these cases, augmentation (agents assisting humans) often outperforms replacement.

## The question CFOs should be asking

The right question isn't "can we automate this?" It's "what is our competitor's cost structure in 18 months if they deploy agents and we don't?"

Knowledge work is about to bifurcate into two cost structures: enterprises with agent layers, and enterprises without them. The firms that act now will set a cost floor their competitors can't match.

Your next hire might still be human. But the decision should be deliberate — not a default.
