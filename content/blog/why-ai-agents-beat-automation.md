For the past decade, enterprises have invested heavily in Robotic Process Automation (RPA). The results have been mixed: great for deterministic, rule-based tasks — fragile for anything requiring contextual judgment.

AI agents are different. They don't follow pre-written scripts. They reason.

## The RPA ceiling

Traditional automation excels at:
- Copying data between systems
- Generating reports on a fixed schedule
- Sending templated emails

But the moment a process requires interpreting a customer's intent, adapting to an unusual edge case, or synthesising information from multiple sources — RPA stalls. Humans step in. The efficiency gains evaporate.

## What agents can do

An AI agent receives a goal, not a script. It can:

1. **Decompose complex tasks** — breaking a broad objective into sub-steps autonomously
2. **Use tools** — calling APIs, writing code, reading documents, browsing the web
3. **Recover from errors** — detecting when an action failed and trying an alternative
4. **Collaborate** — handing off to other agents or escalating to humans when appropriate

Applied to knowledge work, this means an enterprise can deploy agents to handle research, drafting, analysis, and coordination at a fraction of the cost of the equivalent headcount.

## The productivity equation

For a team of 10 senior analysts at £80k each, the total cost including overhead approaches £1.2M per year. A well-deployed agent layer can handle 60–80% of their routine analytical work — at a fraction of that cost — freeing the humans for the 20% that genuinely requires human judgment.

That's not hypothetical. We've seen it happen.

## What this means for enterprises

If your business employs 50+ knowledge workers doing judgment-intensive work — finance, legal, marketing, operations, customer success — you have an agent opportunity worth modelling.

The question isn't whether AI agents will replace parts of these roles. It's whether your competitors will do it before you do.
