We've worked with dozens of enterprises on AI agent deployments. The ones that succeed quickly share common characteristics. The ones that struggle — even with the best technology — tend to be missing a few fundamentals.

This checklist isn't about technology stack or budget. It's about organisational conditions. Use it to honestly assess where you stand.

---

## The 10 signs

### 1. You have high-volume, repeatable knowledge work

The single biggest predictor of successful agent deployment is **volume**. If your team processes 100+ similar tasks per month — contracts, reports, research briefs, analyses, customer queries — you have the throughput to justify and amortise an agent layer.

Low-volume, highly bespoke work is a worse fit, at least initially.

### 2. You can document your current process

Agents follow processes. If your best team members operate largely from undocumented tribal knowledge, you'll need to externalise that first. The good news: this documentation exercise is valuable independent of agents.

Ask yourself: could a competent new hire do this work from written instructions within two weeks? If not, you're not ready — yet.

### 3. You have baseline metrics

You need to know your current performance to prove agent ROI. If you can answer — with data — questions like "how long does this process take today?", "what is our current error rate?", "what is the volume by week?" — you're in a strong position.

If those numbers don't exist, measurement is step one.

### 4. Your leadership has a mandate

Agent deployments that succeed have an executive sponsor with a clear mandate: not "explore AI" but "reduce analyst headcount cost by 30% within 12 months" or "cut contract turnaround from 4 days to 1 day."

Vague mandates produce pilots that never graduate to production.

### 5. You have at least one workflow that is genuinely high-cost and high-friction

The best entry point is a workflow your team actually complains about. Where are the bottlenecks? Where do skilled people spend time on work they find repetitive? High frustration often signals high automation opportunity.

### 6. Your tech stack is API-accessible

Agents integrate with your existing systems. If your core tools — CRM, document management, data warehouse, ticketing system — have APIs or webhooks, integration is straightforward. If everything runs on legacy systems with no programmatic access, expect a higher implementation cost.

### 7. You have someone who owns the outcome

Every successful deployment we've seen has a named owner on the client side: a VP of Operations, Head of Legal Ops, Finance Director. Someone whose personal success metrics include the outcome.

Shared ownership with no clear accountable person is a risk factor.

### 8. You're willing to redesign the workflow, not just automate it

The temptation is to "automate what we do today." The better outcome usually comes from redesigning the workflow around what agents do well. This requires openness to change — including the possibility that some human roles shift significantly.

### 9. Your organisation can handle the change management

Agent deployment is a change management exercise as much as a technology project. The team members whose work changes need to be brought along — ideally positioned as supervisors of the agent layer rather than competitors to it.

Organisations with a history of failed software rollouts due to adoption issues should address this proactively.

### 10. You're planning for the long term

Agents compound. The first six months are about getting the baseline right. The second six months are about expanding coverage. Year two is where the economics become genuinely transformative.

Enterprises that plan for a 12–24 month journey outperform those looking for a 90-day quick win.

## Scoring yourself

**8–10 signs present**: You're ready to move. A strategy engagement will confirm the priority workflows and model the ROI — you should be in production within 6 months.

**5–7 signs present**: You're close. One or two preparatory steps (baseline measurement, process documentation, executive sponsor alignment) will unlock a successful deployment.

**Below 5**: The fundamentals need addressing first. This isn't a technology problem — it's an organisational readiness problem. We can help you build the foundation.
